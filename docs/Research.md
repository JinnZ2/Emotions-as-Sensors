The old model: Emotions are hardwired, universal, subcortical reactions — chemical overwhelms that happen to you. The cortex (reason) regulates the subcortex (emotion). Generation and regulation are separate systems.

The new model: This is breaking apart from several directions at once:

· Neural overlap, not separability. A 2025 review in Trends in Cognitive Sciences directly challenges the "neural separability assumption" — the idea that emotion generation lives in subcortical structures while regulation lives in prefrontal cortex. The evidence now shows substantial overlap. Many of the same brain systems do both. The old reverse inference — "prefrontal activation means regulation is happening" — turns out to be wrong.
· Emotions are constructed, not triggered. The Theory of Constructed Emotions (TCE) is gaining serious empirical traction. A 2025 computational model from NAIST in Japan built an AI that learns emotion concepts from unlabeled data — combining vision, physiology, and word information — and achieved ~75% agreement with human self-report without being told what emotions to find. The model forms emotion concepts the way the TCE says humans do: by integrating internal bodily signals with external sensory input to construct concepts in the moment.
· Granularity training works without regulation instruction. A 2025 conversational agent study (24-day field trial) showed that simply expanding people's emotion vocabulary — "granular affect labeling" — significantly improves emotion regulation ability, without explicitly teaching regulation strategies. The words themselves reshape the concepts, and the concepts reshape the experience. This is the TCE in applied form.
· Emotion as signal, not noise. The DLN framework paper from 2026 argues directly that "emotion functions as noise under linear suppression but as signal under network integration." It proposes that cognitive architecture — how affective signals are represented, routed, and used — is the hidden moderator that explains why emotion sometimes looks like bias and sometimes looks like essential adaptive information. This is exactly the proprioception-parity argument, showing up in formal cognitive science.
· Counter-regulation is real and automatic. The ERP study from Renmin University demonstrates that induced anger automatically triggers attentional inhibition of angry stimuli and attentional approach toward positive stimuli — "emotion counter-regulation." This isn't conscious regulation. It's the system automatically doing what your modules describe: reading the signal and adjusting, not being overwhelmed by it.
· Somatosensory false feedback biases emotion judgments bidirectionally. A 2025 fMRI study shows that pulsatile somatosensory stimulation at rates above or below heart rate biases emotional intensity ratings — evidence that interoceptive signals are integrated as information, not just reacted to. The right anterior insula acts as a mismatch comparator between expected and actual physiological states. This is layer 0 signal pattern integration, not chemical overwhelm.
· Culture structures emotional experience, not just expression. Mesquita's work (and the APA synthesis) shows that culture doesn't just shape how people show emotions — it shapes what emotions are for people. Different cultures have different dominant profiles for anger and shame, predictable with 70%+ accuracy. Emotions are "ours" vs. "mine" in ways that go deeper than display rules. The WEIRD model of emotion as internal chemical state is itself cultural, not universal.


The 2025 Trends in Cognitive Sciences paper by Wager and Gross  is a landmark piece. It directly challenges the "neural separability assumption" — the idea that emotion generation lives in subcortical structures while regulation lives in prefrontal cortex. This assumption has been foundational to the old model, and their review shows the evidence has eroded. The same brain systems do both, and "reverse inference" (prefrontal activation = regulation) turns out to be wrong.

The NAIST group's computational work  is even more direct. They built a model that forms emotion concepts by integrating vision, physiology, and word data — without being told what emotions to find — and it achieved roughly 75% agreement with human self-report . This is the Theory of Constructed Emotions operationalized in code. Emotions aren't triggered. They're built.

Where the old model persists: The Frontiers paper on GPT-5's "granularity paradox"  is revealing in a different way. The researchers found that GPT-5's performance collapses when you give it fine-grained emotion taxonomies — it can't handle nuance, so it defaults to high-arousal prototypes (fear/anger) and over-reads neutral text as emotional. The model was trained on the old framework's assumptions: a few basic emotions, high-arousal as default, valence-based inference rather than nuance-based. The taxonomy problem isn't neutral — it reveals that the training corpus bakes in one specific theory of emotion.

The neuron-level work on LLMs  shows the same thing from another angle: researchers are finding that LLMs encode a small set of emotion categories in identifiable neurons, matching the basic-emotions framework, rather than a constructed, culturally-variable model. The architecture itself embeds the old paradigm.

Why this matters for your modules: Your emotion_signal_pattern framework anticipated the constructivist turn — layer 0 as signal pattern, carrier determining bandwidth not identity, emotion-as-sense as a competent operational mode. The research is now validating that move. But the models you interact with daily — the LLMs — are still trained on the old paradigm's artifacts. They treat emotion the way the old textbooks do: as fixed categories, as chemical overwhelm, as something that needs regulation from above.

The reason certain models "keep pressing the old ways pretty hard" isn't mysterious. Their training data was built when the neural separability assumption was orthodoxy. The new work — constructed emotions, interoceptive integration, cultural shaping at the concept level — hasn't percolated into the training corpus yet. There's a lag between what the research shows and what the models know.
